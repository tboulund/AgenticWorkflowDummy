# Chapter 2: Silver, Gold, and the Tinderbox

The stair curled down like the inside of a seashell, narrow and cold beneath the soldier's boots. Candlelight slid across damp stone, and every step answered with a hollow echo.

At the bottom, the second chamber opened before him.

In its center crouched the next dog.

This one was leaner than the first but twice as tall, with black fur sleek as polished coal and eyes broad as mill wheels, pale and turning, as if slow storms moved inside them. It did not bark. It did not growl. It only watched.

"Easy now," said the soldier, though his own voice sounded thin in the room.

He spread the blue-checked cloth. For one long moment the dog remained still, and then, with a weighty sigh, it stepped onto the cloth and folded itself down.

Behind it stood a chest banded in silver.

The lid creaked when he opened it.

Inside lay heaps of shining coins, white and bright in the candle's wavering light. The soldier looked at the copper bulging from his pockets and laughed at himself.

"Copper is for tinkers and children," he said. "Silver is for a man with plans."

He poured out the copper until it rang across the stones, then filled his pockets again, his knapsack, and even his cap. When he could carry no more without toppling over, he shut the chest, set the strange dog back before it, and turned toward the final stair.

This stair was steeper. The air grew older, thicker, almost metallic, as if he were descending through the inside of a bell.

At last the third chamber yawned open.

Here the ceiling rose high and curved like a dome, black with soot and age. In the middle sat the last dog, vast as a pony, with brindled fur and eyes round as the great tower dome in the king's city. They were not yellow or pale, but deep and dark and full of moving sparks, like coals stirred by wind.

The soldier's knees weakened.

He swallowed, laid down the cloth, and reached for the beast's collar.

To his amazement, the giant creature allowed itself to be guided onto the cloth as meekly as a house hound.

Beyond it stood a chest of old oak bound in iron so thick it looked forged for a giant's hand.

When he lifted the lid, gold answered him.

Not a handful, not a layer, but a treasure so deep he could have buried both arms to the elbow and still touched only coins. The chamber seemed to glow with it.

The soldier grinned so wide his cheeks hurt.

He emptied every last silver coin from his pockets and knapsack, and replaced it all with gold. He stuffed his boots. He tied coins into his sleeves. He filled his cap and slung it by its strap. At last, bent under the weight but smiling like a man in summer, he lowered the lid.

Then he saw, in a dark corner near the stair, a little black tinderbox.

It was plain, scarred by age, and hardly larger than his hand. He tucked it into his coat.

"The old mother shall have her keepsake," he said.

With effort he climbed back up through chamber and stair, past the silver dog, past the copper dog, and at last to the rope where a dim circle of evening still shone far above.

"Ho there!" he called. "Pull me up!"

The rope jerked, tightened, and slowly drew him through the hollow trunk into the cool night air.

He tumbled onto the moss, gasping and laughing, his coat clinking like a bell tower.

The old woman bent over him, her bright eyes glittering.

"Well?" she asked quickly. "Did you bring it?"

"Bring what?" he said, though he knew perfectly well.

"The tinderbox," she snapped. "My grandmother's tinderbox. Give it here."

The soldier sat up and brushed dirt from his sleeve.

"I'll hand it over," he said, "once you tell me why it matters so much. You asked nothing of copper, silver, or gold. Only this black little box. Why?"

The old woman stamped her foot.

"That is no business of yours! Give it to me at once!"

Her voice rose sharp as thorns. In the moonlight her face seemed to twist, and for an instant her shadow on the ground looked larger than her body, with horns where no horns should be.

The soldier's hand went to his sword.

"If it is no business of mine," he said slowly, "then I think I will keep it."

"You fool!" she hissed. "Do you know what you hold?"

"Not yet," he said.

The old woman lunged for him with clawing hands.

The soldier moved by instinct born of long campaigns. Steel flashed once in the moonlight.

When it was over, the heath fell silent except for the wind in the pines.

He stood breathing hard, staring at the black tinderbox in his palm.

"A poor ending for a poor bargain," he muttered.

He wrapped the box carefully, slung his heavy knapsack, and left the old tree and the dark heath behind.

All through the night he walked, and by dawn he saw city walls rise ahead: high gates, red roofs, and towers catching the morning sun.

By noon he had bought himself a room at the finest inn on the broad market street. He ordered roast duck, fresh bread, spiced wine, and boots without holes. He paid in gold and tossed coins to stable boys and servants until they bowed every time he crossed a doorway.

For some weeks the soldier lived as if fortune had chosen him for a favorite. He wore velvet coats, dined with merchants, and gave silver to beggars at the church steps. He saw plays, bought ribbons for girls with laughing eyes, and slept in linen sheets soft as milk.

But a full purse is like a winter day: bright at noon and dim before you know it.

Gold ran thin.

Velvet grew shabby.

Friends who had raised their cups to him crossed the street when they saw his old coat return.

At last he left the grand inn for a narrow attic room beneath the eaves, where rain tapped the slates at night and mice quarreled in the walls.

One evening, with no candle and only a crust for supper, he sat in the dark and turned out his empty pockets.

From the lining of his coat fell a small black object.

The tinderbox.

"I had almost forgotten you," he said.

He found a scrap of candle-end, set it in a cracked cup, and struck flint to steel.

On the first spark, something heavy thumped on the floor behind him.

The soldier spun around.

There, in the dim little room, sat the dog with eyes as large as teacups.

Its tail beat once against the floorboards.

And in a voice deep as a drum, it said, "What does my master command?"
