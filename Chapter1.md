# Chapter 1: The Road, the Witch, and the Hollow Tree

Long ago, when kings wore crowns of hammered gold and soldiers walked home with dust on their boots, there came a man along the high road. He had a broad back, a red coat faded by weather, and a sword at his side that had seen more rain than battle. In his pocket he had only a few coins, in his knapsack a crust of bread, and in his heart the cheerful thought that the world was still wide and he had two good legs to cross it with.

Toward evening, the road bent through a heath where heather grew in purple clumps and crooked pines leaned as if listening to the wind. The sun sank low, and the shadows stretched long. Just as the soldier began to wonder where he might find supper and a bed of straw, he saw an old woman standing by a milestone.

She was bent almost in two, with a black hood, a chin like a hook, and eyes bright as wet pebbles. Her hands were thin and restless, and on one finger she wore a ring set with a stone the color of smoke.

"Good evening, soldier," she said, in a voice that seemed at once cracked and smooth.

"Good evening, mother," he answered, for he was polite even when he was hungry.

"You carry a fine sword," she said. "And if I am not mistaken, your purse is light."

"You are not mistaken," said the soldier. "The purse is so light it might blow away in a strong wind."

The old woman laughed, and the laugh made the pine needles shiver.

"Then perhaps we can help one another. Do you see that great tree yonder?"

She pointed with her bony hand. At first the soldier thought he saw only a massive oak, old as a church, with roots like coiled serpents and a trunk thick enough for three men to hide behind. But when he came closer, he noticed a dark opening near the base, half-hidden by moss and fern.

"It is hollow all the way down," said the old woman. "Inside are three chambers, one beneath another. In each chamber stands a chest of coins. In the first chest is copper, in the second silver, and in the third gold that could make a beggar richer than a prince."

The soldier whistled softly.

"And why," he asked, "has no one taken these chests before me?"

The old woman smiled, but not kindly.

"Because each chest is watched by a dog, and no ordinary dog at that. In the first room sits one with eyes as large as teacups. In the second, eyes as large as mill wheels. In the third, eyes round and wide as the great tower dome in the king's city. Their gaze can freeze the marrow in a coward's bones."

"Then I suppose you need a brave man," said the soldier.

"Exactly. You shall have as much money as your pockets can hold. All I ask in return is that you bring me an old tinderbox lying in the deepest chamber. It belonged to my grandmother, and I have reason to want it back."

"A tinderbox?" said the soldier. "Only that?"

"Only that," she said.

From her apron she drew a blue-checked cloth.

"Take this. Spread it on the floor before each chest. Set the dog upon it, and it will not harm you while you gather your coins."

The soldier turned the cloth over in his hands. It looked ordinary enough, though the threads shimmered for a moment when the last sunlight touched them.

"And how do I come up again?" he asked.

"I shall tie a rope around your waist and lower you down. When you have what you want, call out, and I will pull you up."

The soldier stood very still. The wind passed through the heath with a sound like distant surf. He had met tricksters before, and flatterers, and liars who smiled with too many teeth. Yet hunger is a sharp adviser, and empty pockets are persuasive.

"Very well," he said at last. "Tie the rope."

The old woman moved quickly for one so bent. She knotted the rope with surprising strength, then handed him a wax candle and a flint.

"Mind your step," she said softly. "The dark below is older than the road above."

The soldier crouched at the opening. Cool air rose from the hollow trunk, smelling of damp earth, iron, and something else he could not name. He took a breath, wedged his boots against the bark, and began to descend.

Down he went, past twisted roots and black walls slick with age. The light from above shrank to a pale coin. Soon he touched solid ground.

He struck flint to steel. The candle flame leapt up, thin and trembling, and cast long shadows across a low stone chamber.

There, in the first room, sat a dog.

It was broad as a calf, with fur the color of ash and eyes as round as teacups, shining yellow in the candlelight.

The soldier's hand tightened on his sword-hilt, but he remembered the cloth. Steady as if setting down a sleeping child, he spread the blue-checked square on the floor and lifted the dog onto it. The creature blinked once, yawned, and watched him in silence.

At the center of the room stood an iron-bound chest. The soldier lifted the lid. Copper coins flashed like new pennies under rain.

He laughed under his breath.

"Well now," he murmured, "this road may turn kinder yet."

With both hands he scooped coins into his pockets, then into his knapsack, and still the chest seemed hardly emptier than before.

When he had taken enough to make his belt sag, he closed the lid, set the dog back before it, and moved toward the stairway that spiraled down to the second chamber.

The candle fluttered.

From below came the faint scrape of claws on stone.

And as the soldier lowered his foot onto the first step, he told himself he had seen strange things in war and stranger things on lonely roads.

Yet in truth, for the first time that day, he felt the hairs on his neck rise.

He lifted the candle higher and descended into the dark.
